D I S N E Y' S 12
Principle's of
Animations

Squash and Stretch: all objects have a certain degree of flexibility and will change shape when subjected to outside forces or motions. This idea aids in creating the impression of weight and motion for a character or item.
Anticipation: To get the audience ready for a specific action or movement, employ anticipation. It can be used to increase the plausibility of an action and to establish expectation.
Staging: A clean and understandable presentation of an idea or story. It entails focusing the audience's attention on aspects of the animation by using composition, lighting, and camera angles.
Straight Ahead Action and Pose to Pose: These are two various methods of animation. Pose-to-pose animation involves planning out the main poses or actions and then filling in the in-between frames as opposed to straight-ahead action, which involves animating frame by frame from beginning to end. Depending on the circumstance, either approach can be used successfully.
Follow Through and Overlapping Action: This principle relates to the notion that not all components of a character or object will move simultaneously or uniformly. There will be follow-through and overlapping action because of some parts moving more quickly than others.
Slow In and Out: Using this principle, an action is started and ended gradually to produce a movement that looks more natural. Giving an object or character the appearance of weight and mass is helpful.
Arcs: Are used to produce motion that is more fluid and realistic-looking. To make their animations appear more realistic, animators use the idea that most movements in real life have an arc or curve.
Secondary Action: This idea entails adding extra gestures or actions to a character's main action to support it. It can be used to give an animation more complexity and depth.
Timing: It is important in animation because it contributes to the rhythm and pacing of the work. Timing of important poses must also take movement and action speed into account.
Exaggeration: Is used to highlight specific elements of an animation, such as facial expressions, body language, or feelings. It can also be used to add humor or entertainment value to an animation.
Solid Drawing: Gives the appearance of three-dimensional space on a two-dimensional surface. It necessitates a thorough understanding of movement, perspective, and anatomy.
Appeal: It entails developing settings and characters that are captivating to viewers and visually appealing.
